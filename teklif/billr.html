01 / Context

Two customers, both 3PL.
Signal or coincidence?

Billr is a Vienna-based, 2024-founded startup building custom billing infrastructure for companies whose pricing doesn't fit Chargebee, Stripe Billing, or Zuora. Two live references — Quivo (€50M European 3PL, 800+ customers) and Ecomflow (China-direct 3PL, 150+ brands, 25K+ daily shipments) — both land in the same vertical: third-party logistics.

Open Question 1

ICP clarity

Is 3PL the wedge, or a coincidence of who the founder knew? Which sub-segment — European mid-market, APAC D2C fulfillment, freight forwarders?

Open Question 2

Offer clarity

"Custom billing infrastructure" vs. "replace your billing engineer" vs. "unlock pricing you can't bill today" — which angle actually converts?

Open Question 3

Channel clarity

Does cold outbound produce conversations with CFOs, Heads of Finance, CTOs — and at what response rate?

The reframe: rather than commit to one ICP and message up front, we run the sprint as a structured experiment. It produces pipeline and ICP evidence simultaneously. Every reply, every objection, every meeting becomes data toward Billr's next-round ICP slide.

02 / Tibexa's Recommendation

Logistics should be the vertical.
Horizontal expansion comes later.

The multi-hypothesis sprint is structured to test the founder's preference for breadth. But Tibexa's professional view is that Billr should concentrate the next 18–24 months in logistics and treat horizontal moves as deliberate, later-stage decisions. Five reasons:

1

Market size is already more than sufficient

~340,000 3PL firms globally (IBISWorld / Speed Commerce 2024). US alone ~73K. Global 3PL market $1.41T in 2025, projected $1.54T in 2026. Capturing 0.1% of the signal-filtered SAM alone produces Series-B-grade revenue.

2

Operational complexity compounds non-linearly

Three ICP languages, three sales motions (CFO vs. CTO vs. Product), three implementation patterns, three scattered marketing presences. At 2–10 employees, this is a fatal distraction.

3

Logistics has defensible moats that horizontal doesn't

In horizontal usage-based, Billr competes with Metronome, Orb, Lago, Togai, Stripe Billing — all well-funded. In logistics billing, incumbents are Extensiv, Hopstack, and spreadsheets. More winnable field.

4

Vertical language is a sales unlock

Logistics CFOs speak pallets, pick fees, accessorials, peak-season surcharges, DIM-weight gotchas. A vendor using that language in the first email has a massive edge over generic "usage-based billing infrastructure" pitches.

5

The horizontal option stays open later

Logistics-first doesn't close off usage-based SaaS — it sequences it. Once Billr has 15–25 logistics customers, expansion happens without duress or fragmentation.

What this means for the sprint

We run all three hypotheses — but pre-weighted toward H1 logistics. Month-2 and Month-3 decision gates push remaining budget toward logistics unless H2 shows unusually strong signal. H3 only runs in the Large package as a directional probe.

What we don't pre-filter

No employee-count ceiling — a 30-person startup and a 2,000-person logistics group can have the same billing pain. No geography filter — English-language reach; the heat-map from replies reveals where the wedge lives. No SaaS-only bias — Quivo (operator) and Ecomflow (3PL-as-service) both work.

Explicitly out of scope

Pre-seed / <$500K ARR — can't afford custom. Consumer subscription — Stripe/Recurly dominate. Regulated banking / payments — compliance bar too high for a 2024-founded vendor. Public-sector billing — RFP-driven, wrong channel for outbound.

04 / Value Angles & Messages

Four angles.
Four buyer psychologies.

The four pitches we A/B test
1

Escape the middleware tax

"Chargebee/Stripe Billing forces you to build a middleware layer. We replace that layer." → CTO / VP Eng

2

Stop the revenue leak

"If a price isn't entered, you silently lose money. We flag it in real time, not month-end." (Quivo angle) → CFO

3

Billing in hours, not days

"Monthly billing takes 12+ hours with overnight test runs. We compress to 1 hour with zero test runs." → Head of Finance / Ops

4

Price anything you can imagine

"Volume tiers, customer-specific overrides, mixed models, currency conversion — model it, don't build it." → Product / commercial

Operating principle

Each segment gets 2–3 message variants mapping to angles above. One primary touch + one follow-up per contact. Month-2 A/B testing narrows variants from 6 to 2–3; Month-3 concentrates on winners.

Why these angles and not others: Angles #2 and #3 have concrete numbers attached (1,170 price sheets → 1 hour; 12+ hours → 1 hour). Angles #1 and #4 are aspirational — broader appeal, thinner proof. Early reply data will tell us which converts. We bias the first round toward the concrete ones, because case-study-anchored pitches start closer to "believable" on the cold-email curve.

Response-rate baseline: 0.35% positive-reply-to-contact ratio is our conservative anchor for high-volume segmented B2B outbound. Observed range for comparable operations in 2026 sits at 0.35%–0.80%. If the sprint outperforms, numbers scale linearly — a 0.55% rate on the Medium package produces ~412 replies instead of 262.

09 / Sales Cycle & Timeline

Most closes land after the sprint ends.
By design.

Custom billing infrastructure is a considered B2B purchase — CFO buy-in, security review, technical evaluation, SOC2 questions. Cold contact to live customer typically takes 3–6 months. The sprint produces pipeline and ICP evidence; ARR materializes across the following two quarters.
